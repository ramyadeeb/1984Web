Dear Investor As the TikTok drama unfolds, it’s easy to write off the Trump administration’s threat to shut off the video app as yet another episode in the US-China saga. But a deeper look reveals a broader trend. In June, following a border skirmish with next-door China, India retaliated in part by shutting off TikTok along with 58 other Chinese apps. And in July Slack decided to pursue antitrust action against Microsoft over the bundling of its Teams product not in the US, home to both companies, but rather in the European Union where regulators look more harshly on tech giants. These stories may seem like disparate anecdotes, but they highlight a shared underpinning: the internet is becoming fragmented. A combination of government and company politics are creating four disparate internet models across the world: a US internet marked by freewheeling and innovation, a robust Chinese ecosystem behind a closed off wall, a European one hampered by regulation, and an emerging Indian model dominated by Jio that may soon be followed in other developing countries. This fragmentation may serve various political interests but is costly to businesses and consumers. It is the subject of our newsletter for this month. India, Jio, and the Four Internets https://stratechery.com/2020/india-jio-and-the-four-internets/ In this thoughtful piece, Ben Thompson articulates the emergence of the four models of operating the internet and the pros and cons for each. Ben’s worry is not the US shutting off TikTok, which he considers a “response in kind” to China’s 23 years of walled off internet. Rather it is the increased splintering in the non-China Internet, particularly the emergence of a European model that is “the worst of all possible outcomes.” 2. The TikTok War https://stratechery.com/2020/the-tiktok-war/ In this thought-provoking article, Stratechery’s Ben Thompson argues that the Chinese government has long perceived itself to be in an ideological struggle with the US. The Trump administration is concerned about the data that China could capture via Americans’ widespread use of TikTok. Thompson points out that the reverse flow of information poses a greater danger: TikTok’s algorithm gives China the capability to clandestinely influence millions of Americans in a contest for America’s hearts and minds. 3.
