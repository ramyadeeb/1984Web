Portfolio Update
During the Great Recession, the advice we gave all our startups was to preserve cash. But this crisis requires a more nuanced response. At 1984 Ventures, we have instituted an operational framework based on impact to demand and cash position to guide our portfolio companies: Startups where demand has struggled are aggressively preserving cash (and retaining existing customers), while businesses with increased demand are focused on opportunistics growth.

This month we made one new investment (to be announced) and participated in two follow-on rounds in portfolio companies: Flowspace, which automates eCommerce fulfilment, and Kyte, which provides on-demand car rentals delivered directly to consumers. We have always been impressed with their execution and growth, and even more so during the crisis.

The Payment Protection Program
On April 4th the government rolled out the Payment Protection Program (Which was fully exhausted by April 16th). We observed an intriguing association: Startups with strong cash reserves were much more successful in receiving the loan than startups with little cash. The better-funded startups tended to have a dedicated CFO, stronger relationships with lenders, and were faster in applying. The point of this story is not to focus on the teething pains of the bailout program (or to vindicate Milton Friedman, who famously quipped that “nothing is more permanent than a temporary government program”). Rather it is to highlight that regardless of government intervention, crises often accelerate fates that were already in the making.
